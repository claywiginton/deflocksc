What to say — a 60-second script

“My name is [your name] and I live in [your neighborhood]. I'm asking the Council to require a public vote, a warrant, a 21-day data limit, and published audits before any license-plate cameras operate here. [One sentence — why this matters to you.] These cameras went up with no vote; please give the public a say. Thank you.”

[ THE ONE THING TO UNDERSTAND FIRST ]

The live lever right now is local

The South Carolina legislature adjourned for the year on May 14, 2026. The two strongest state bills didn't pass, so the state fight resets to the session that begins in January 2027. That makes the state a plan to prepare — and makes city and county councils the only lever that can move this year. They control their own police and sheriff contracts and can act in any month. That's where the energy goes now.

Start here — the most winnable first target

Greenville County Council

In 2026 a new, reform-minded majority took over the council and elected Benton Blount as chairman — a body that ran on limited government, spending accountability, and "no one asked us." That is the ideal audience for an un-voted, tax-funded surveillance grid that hands residents' movements to federal agencies. The council can pass an oversight ordinance — or decline to fund the cameras — with a simple majority vote, no state action required.

How it works: the council's twelve members each represent one district and meet the 1st and 3rd Tuesday of the month at County Square, 301 University Ridge. Meetings include a public-comment period. Find your district's member on the county's official roster, then use the email template below.

Who holds the switch

Four levers, in order of how fast they can move. The local bodies act on their own timeline; the state play is a 2027 effort — but your champions there already exist.

LOCAL — CAN ACT THIS YEAR

County · fastest win

Greenville County Council

Chairman Benton Blount & the 2026 reform majority · twelve single-member districts

The lever
Can pass an ALPR oversight ordinance, or decline to fund the Sheriff's cameras, by majority vote.
What moves them
Fiscal accountability · "no public vote" · taxpayer money · local control · federal & out-of-state access to your data (ICE, DEA).
The ask
Adopt the model ordinance: public vote, warrant for searches, 21-day retention, published audits, no immigration use.

Greenville County Sheriff

Sheriff Hobart Lewis · runs the county's day-to-day camera use

The lever
Sets retention, access, and audit practice for the Sheriff's Office deployment.
What moves them
Effective tools, but also liability and audit failures — and not becoming the next wrongful-stop headline.
The ask
Publish the contract and audit logs; adopt warrant + retention limits; end out-of-state and federal bulk sharing.

City · controls the city PD contract

Greenville City Council & Mayor

Mayor Knox White (since 1995) & council · the city's budget funds Flock ALPR & facial recognition

The lever
Owns the Greenville PD Flock contract — can add oversight rules or let it lapse.
What moves them
Greenville's reputation · legal liability (the two sisters held at gunpoint on a false hit are suing) · a program that doesn't deliver the safety it promises.
The ask
An oversight ordinance and a public hearing before any renewal — or let the contract lapse.

County · the pressure point

Spartanburg County

County Council & Sheriff's Office — post–Chuck Wright

The lever
Same as Greenville County: council funding + sheriff operations.
What moves them
Credibility. The sheriff who built Spartanburg's camera program, Chuck Wright, pled guilty to federal felonies in October 2025 — no one voted on the dragnet he stood up.
The ask

[ WHERE THE BILLS STAND ]

The four state bills — and which one to push

Four ALPR bills were filed; all sit in committee, and the 2025–26 session adjourned without passing any of them — so the fight resets in January 2027. Read them, then ask the legislators you found above to champion the strongest, H.4675.

H.4675Strongest — back this one

Bans cloud storage, AI tracking & federal access

Bars third-party cloud storage, prohibits AI vehicle tracking beyond plates, caps retention at 21 days, requires warrants, and blocks federal access. If it passed, every existing Flock contract in SC would be void.

In House Judiciary · last action 1/13/2026 · Read the bill ↗

S.447Furthest along — but weak

Regulates plate readers statewide

Cleared Senate Judiciary and awaits a floor vote. Sets 90-day retention and written-policy rules — but leaves cloud storage, federal sharing, and AI tracking untouched, and authorizes the SLED database now being challenged in court.

Awaiting Senate floor · favorable 4/9/2026 · Read the bill ↗

H.3155Stalled

Regulates ALPR deployment & data sharing

Sits in House Judiciary with no action since referral. Would require transparency about sharing data with other agencies, including federal law enforcement.

Subject: Please put ALPR surveillance oversight on the agenda Dear Councilmember [Name], I'm a resident of District [#] in [your town]. I'm writing because there are now more than 400 automated license-plate reader (ALPR) cameras across the Upstate — including here in Greenville County — logging where every driver goes, with no public vote, no warrant requirement, and no published rules on who can search the data. This is government surveillance of everyone, all the time, that no one voted for. Some of these cameras were first bought with federal forfeiture money that bypassed the normal budget, and the data can be queried by out-of-state police and federal agencies like ICE and the DEA. I'm asking the Council to take up a simple oversight ordinance: require a public vote before any ALPR program, a warrant for searches, a 21-day limit on data retention, published audits of who runs searches and why, and a ban on using the system for immigration enforcement. Subject: Oversight (or a hearing) before Greenville renews Flock Dear Mayor White and Members of Council, I'm a Greenville resident. The city's budget funds Flock automated license-plate readers and related surveillance technology, yet residents have never had a public hearing on the rules governing it — who can search it, how long our movements are stored, or how errors are handled. This isn't hypothetical. Two Greenville-area sisters were handcuffed at gunpoint after a camera wrongly flagged their car as stolen, and they are now suing. Cities from Austin to Oshkosh have cancelled their programs after audits found searches with no documented justification. Before any renewal, I'm asking the Council to hold a public hearing and adopt an oversight ordinance — a warrant requirement, a short retention limit, published audits, and no immigration or out-of-state bulk sharing — or to let the contract lapse. Thank you for your service to Greenville, [Your name] [Address]

The model ordinance

A drop-in framework a council can adopt. It doesn't outright ban the cameras — it restores the consent, particularity, and accountability the Constitution requires.

Read the model ordinance provisions
  1. Public authorization. No agency may operate, expand, or renew an ALPR program without a recorded public vote of the governing body, following a published hearing.
  2. Warrant for searches. Stored ALPR data may be searched only pursuant to a warrant supported by probable cause, except in narrowly defined, documented exigent circumstances.
  3. Short retention. Non-hit data is automatically and permanently deleted within 21 days; there is no bulk archival of the movements of un-suspected residents.
  4. No immigration or civil use. The system may not be used for immigration enforcement, routine traffic/code enforcement, or civil matters.
  5. No federal or out-of-state bulk sharing. Data is not streamed to a national network or made queryable by outside agencies without a specific, logged legal process.
  6. Published audits. A quarterly audit of every search — who ran it, the case number, and the justification — is reviewed by an independent body and published.
  7. Transparency. The contract, camera locations, retention setting, and access list are public records posted online.
  8. Sunset. Authorization expires and must be publicly re-approved every two years, with the audit record in hand.

This mirrors the standard of the state Community Data Protection Act (H.4675) at the local level — a template you can hand a council member today.
