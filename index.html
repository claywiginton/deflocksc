[ NOT YOUR GRANDFATHER'S PLATE READER ]

What a Flock camera actually does

Every car, all the time

It photographs and logs every vehicle that passes — not suspects, everyone — 24/7, whether or not any crime is suspected.

An AI fingerprint

Software catalogs plate, make, model, color, bumper stickers, dents, and roof racks — a searchable fingerprint of your vehicle and your habits.

A national cloud network

Data streams to Flock's servers and a nationwide database. Thousands of agencies — and federal ones like ICE and the DEA — can query it.

A map of your life

Aggregated, it reveals where you sleep, worship, protest, and take your children — a detailed record of movement, held for months or years.

[ THE FOUNDING PRINCIPLE ]

The founders fought a war over this exact thing.

The general, suspicionless search was not a footnote to the Revolution — it was the spark. James Otis spent five hours arguing against it before a Boston court in 1761; a young John Adams sat watching, and marked that day as the birth of American independence.

[ 1761 · BOSTON ]

The writ of assistance: a general warrant

  1. Search at willNo probable cause, no specific target — officers could enter and search any home or shop.
  2. Everyone, indefinitelyIt named no person and no place; it swept in the innocent alongside the guilty.
  3. Perpetual“There is no return” — it never expired within a reign and answered to no one.
  4. No accountability“Every man may reign secure in his petty tyranny,” Otis warned.

[ THE REMEDY THEY WROTE ]

The Fourth Amendment exists to forbid exactly this

“The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated … and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause … particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized.”

Read it against a Flock camera:

  • No probable cause — it searches everyone who drives past.
  • No particular place or person — the opposite of what the amendment demands.
  • No warrant, no oath, no judge — the data is collected first and questioned never.

[ THE DECLARATION'S OWN WORDS ]

A standing army — in peacetime, without our consent

“He has erected a multitude of New Offices, and sent hither swarms of Officers to harass our people, and eat out their substance.”

A network of cameras on every thoroughfare is the modern swarm of officers — a permanent watch that costs the public and answers to no one.

“He has kept among us, in times of Peace, Standing Armies without the Consent of our legislatures.”

Cameras that never sleep, deployed with no public vote, are a standing surveillance force stationed against a peaceful population.

[ THE TRADE DOESN'T EVEN PAY OFF ]

And it doesn't deliver the safety it promises

Flock lies to the officials it sells to

In Oshkosh, WI, Flock told the council its system built no movement “heat map.” The next morning the city learned that was false and reconvened to cancel.

Even best-in-class rules failed

Austin had one of the strictest ALPR policies in the country. Its own audit found 20%+ of database searches lacked any documented justification. The city cancelled.

The custodians aren't spotless

Spartanburg County's Flock deployment was overseen by Sheriff Chuck Wright — who pled guilty to federal felonies, including wire fraud, in October 2025.

The system itself can be hacked

Independent researchers found dozens of vulnerabilities in Flock cameras. A similar Iranian ALPR network was hacked and used to target individuals.

[ YOU WOULD NOT BE OUT ON A LIMB ]

The law — and the country — are turning

Norfolk, VA 2024

A court ruled that pulling location data from the city's Flock cameras was a warrantless search under the Fourth Amendment — likening the network to a tracking device.

Carpenter v. U.S. 2018

The Supreme Court held that long-term, comprehensive location tracking implicates the Fourth Amendment even for movements in public.

United States v. Jones 2012

The Court held that attaching a GPS tracker to follow a vehicle's movements is a search — the precedent Norfolk applied to ALPRs.

[ THE REALISTIC PATH ]

How we actually win this in South Carolina

LOCAL

Let the contract lapse, or pass an ordinance

City & county councils control the police contract directly — the fastest lever, no state action required.

STATE

Back H.4675 — the strongest bill filed

Bans cloud-stored ALPR data and AI vehicle tracking, caps retention at 21 days, requires a warrant. Stuck in House Judiciary — it needs a champion.

WATCH

S.447 is furthest along

Cleared a Senate Judiciary subcommittee. It regulates rather than bans — push floor amendments to strengthen it; don't let it become cover for the status quo.

Honest note: none of the four bills now pending is a clean, outright ban. Closing that gap is where a Greenville-area champion can lead.
