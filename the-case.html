THE FOUNDING CASE  ·  IN THEIR OWN WORDS

This is exactly
what we fought
against.

Dragnet license-plate surveillance is not a new question. The general, suspicionless search — the government watching everyone in case anyone is guilty — is the precise abuse that lit the fuse of the American Revolution. The founders named it, argued it for hours in open court, wrote it into their list of grievances against a king, and then built a constitutional wall to keep it out forever. Here is that record, in their own words — and how a Flock camera walks straight through it.

[ 1761 · BOSTON · THE SPARK ]

The writ of assistance: the search that started a revolution

A writ of assistance was a general search warrant. It named no suspect, no house, and no crime. It let a customs officer enter any home, shop, or warehouse he pleased, at any time, and rummage for anything — and it never expired. In February 1761, a Boston lawyer named James Otis stood before the Superior Court and argued for nearly five hours that such a writ was void. He lost the case. He won the argument that became a country.

  1. Suspicion of no oneIt required no probable cause and no specific target. Everyone was a subject of the search; no one was accused.
  2. Everyone, everywhereIt named no person and no place. It swept the innocent in with the guilty as a matter of design, not accident.
  3. Never expiringThe writ ran for the life of the sovereign and answered to no court. There was no return, no review, no end.
  4. No accountability“Every one with this writ may be a tyrant,” Otis warned — a petty tyranny that no judge or citizen could check.
“Then and there the child Independence was born.”

John Adams, a young lawyer, sat in that courtroom and took the notes we still read today. Otis, he wrote, “was a flame of fire.” Half a century later Adams marked that February afternoon as the true birth of the Revolution: “American independence was then and there born.” The fight was not first about tea or taxes. It was about the general search.Source: John Adams, letters & his “Abstract of the Argument” (1761), Founders Online / Legal Papers of John Adams, vol. 2.

Read the writ of assistance against a Flock camera:

  • Suspicion of no one — the camera photographs every car, accused of nothing.
  • Everyone, everywhere — it names no person and no place; that is the entire point of a dragnet.
  • Never expiring — the record is kept for months or years and is searchable long after the fact.
  • No accountability — no warrant, no judge, no oath, and in most places no audit of who searches it.

[ THE LAW THE FOUNDERS STUDIED ]

The general warrant was already on trial — and losing

The colonists were not inventing a grievance. Across the Atlantic, English courts were striking down the exact instrument the founders feared, in cases every educated American read. These decisions — Wilkes, Entick, and Pitt's speech on the floor of Parliament — are the direct ancestors of the Fourth Amendment. The Supreme Court still cites them to explain what the framers meant.

Wilkes v. Wood 1763

The Crown used a single general warrant to hunt the author of an anonymous pamphlet — searching at least five houses and arresting some forty-nine people, none of them named in it. Lord Camden ruled the warrant illegal: such a power “may affect the person and property of every man in this kingdom, and is totally subversive of the liberty of the subject.” Scholars call it the most famous case in late-eighteenth-century America.

Entick v. Carrington 1765

King's messengers broke into John Entick's house on a general warrant and seized his papers. Lord Camden's judgment became a monument of liberty: “By the laws of England, every invasion of private property, be it ever so minute, is a trespass.” Government, he held, must point to specific legal authority for every intrusion. The U.S. Supreme Court calls it a guide to understanding the Fourth Amendment itself.

William Pitt in Parliament 1763

Opposing a tax that authorized warrantless searches, Pitt gave the age its defining image of the private realm: “The poorest man may in his cottage bid defiance to all the force of the Crown. It may be frail — its roof may shake — the wind may blow through it — the storm may enter, the rain may enter — but the King of England cannot enter.”

Every one of these cases turned on a single principle: the state may not search you unless it can say, in advance and under oath, whom it suspects and why. A camera that logs every plate on the road satisfies none of it — it is the general warrant the English courts had already condemned, now running automatically and forever.

[ THE DECLARATION OF INDEPENDENCE · 1776 ]

The grievances they went to war over — read against the surveillance state

The Declaration is, above all, a bill of particulars: a numbered indictment of specific abuses. Strip away the eighteenth-century spelling and a startling number of them describe, almost exactly, the machinery of modern mass surveillance — a permanent force of watchers, stationed among a peaceful people, answering to no vote and no court. Here are the founders' own charges, in the original words, beside the thing they now describe.

“He has erected a multitude of New Offices, and sent hither swarms of Officers to harass our people, and eat out their substance.”Declaration of Independence, 1776

The parallel

A network of hundreds of cameras on the Upstate's roads is the modern swarm of officers — a permanent watch erected without our leave, funded from our own pockets, harassing the innocent as readily as the guilty and answering to no one.

“He has kept among us, in times of Peace, Standing Armies without the Consent of our legislatures.”Declaration of Independence, 1776

The parallel

Cameras that never sleep, deployed with no public vote, are a standing surveillance force garrisoned against a peaceful population. The founders feared a standing army precisely because a permanent, ever-present power watching the people is the tool of despotism, not safety.

“For quartering large bodies of armed troops among us.”Declaration of Independence, 1776

The parallel

The quartering the founders resented was the intrusion of the state's eyes and force into ordinary life. ALPR sensors quartered on every thoroughfare do the same work without a barracks — a piece of the government stationed permanently within view of your driveway, your church, your children's school.

“He has combined with others to subject us to a jurisdiction foreign to our constitution … giving his Assent to their Acts of pretended Legislation.”Declaration of Independence, 1776

The parallel

A plate photographed in Greenville flows into a national cloud queryable by thousands of agencies — out-of-state police, ICE, the DEA — jurisdictions foreign to our community, under rules our legislature never wrote and never voted on.

“For depriving us in many cases, of the benefits of Trial by Jury.”Declaration of Independence, 1776

The parallel

An algorithm flags your car; an officer acts on the flag. There is no accuser to face, no warrant to challenge, no hearing before the fact. When the machine is wrong — as it was for two Greenville sisters held at gunpoint over a misread plate — the “trial” happens on the roadside, at the wrong end of a drawn weapon.

“For transporting us beyond Seas to be tried for pretended offences.”Declaration of Independence, 1776

The parallel

Your movements, captured here, can be pulled and used against you somewhere you have never been, by an agency you have never heard of, for conduct that is lawful where you live. The record travels; you have no say in where it lands.

The Declaration ends its list with a warning that reads like a mission statement: a government that will not answer to the people it watches has broken the compact. “A Prince, whose character is thus marked by every act which may define a Tyrant, is unfit to be the ruler of a free people.”

[ THE WALL THEY BUILT ]

From Mason to Madison: the remedy they wrote into the law

The founders did not merely complain about the general search. They abolished it — deliberately, in writing, in the most protected law they could draft. The line runs straight from Otis's courtroom to the Bill of Rights.

1

George Mason — Virginia, 1776

Weeks before the Declaration, Mason's Virginia Declaration of Rights condemned the general warrant by name: those “whereby an officer or messenger may be commanded to search suspected places without evidence of a fact committed, or to seize any person or persons not named … are grievous and oppressive, and ought not to be granted.” Virginia Declaration of Rights, Article X.

2

John Adams — Massachusetts, 1780

The man who witnessed Otis wrote the search-and-seizure clause of the Massachusetts Constitution — the direct model for the federal Fourth Amendment — guaranteeing every citizen the right “to be secure from all unreasonable searches, and seizures, of his person, his houses, his papers, and all his possessions.” Massachusetts Constitution, Article XIV.

3

James Madison — the Bill of Rights, 1789

Pressed by Patrick Henry and the anti-federalists, Madison drafted the amendment that made the ban national and permanent. Ratified in 1791, it is the founders' final word on the general search.

“The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause, supported by Oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized.” The Fourth Amendment to the Constitution of the United States, ratified 1791.

Every clause is a rule a Flock camera breaks:

  • “Probable cause” — a dragnet has none; it searches everyone who drives past.
  • “Oath or affirmation” — no officer swears anything; a machine simply records.
  • “Particularly describing the place … and the persons” — the camera describes no one and everyone. It is the exact opposite of particularity, which is the whole safeguard.
  • “Shall not be violated” — the data is gathered first and questioned never; the wall is simply walked around.

[ IN THEIR OWN WORDS ]

The founders saw this coming

Not one voice, but a chorus — English jurists, revolutionary lawyers, and the architects of the Constitution — all warning against the same thing: a government that watches its people by default, and the slow, quiet way free nations lose their liberty.

[ THE INFRINGEMENT, IN DETAIL ]

Which rights this breaks — and how

Mass license-plate surveillance is not a single violation. It cuts against a whole architecture of protected liberty at once — the freedom from search, the freedom to associate and worship and protest unwatched, the right to due process, and the most basic republican principle of all: that a free people is not policed without its consent.

Fourth Amendment · Search & Seizure

The suspicionless dragnet

The founding rule: no search without probable cause, an oath, and a warrant that names a particular person or place.

ALPR inverts every element. It searches everyone, on no suspicion, with no warrant and no oath, describing no one in particular. Worse, it is retroactive: because the data is stored, the state can search your past movements for a reason it invents later. In Carpenter v. United States (2018) and United States v. Jones (2012), the Supreme Court recognized that long-term, comprehensive location tracking is a search under the Fourth Amendment — even of movements made in public. A 2024 Virginia court applied that reasoning directly to a Flock network, likening it to a tracking device.

First Amendment · Association & Belief

The chilling of a free people

The founding rule: citizens may gather, worship, petition, and dissent without the government cataloguing who showed up.

A record of where your car goes is a record of who you are: the church or mosque you attend, the union hall, the protest, the recovery meeting, the gun show, the political rally, the reporter's driveway. When people know their movements are logged, they self-censor — they stop going. The Kansas case of an ALPR used to pursue a man who merely criticized police in an op-ed is the chilling effect made literal. A surveillance grid that maps association is a standing threat to the freedoms the First Amendment exists to guarantee.

Fifth & Fourteenth Amendments · Due Process

Guilt by algorithm

The founding rule: no one is deprived of liberty without notice, a fair hearing, and the chance to confront the evidence.

An ALPR “hit” is an accusation generated by software, acted on before any human judgment. There is no accuser to cross-examine and no warrant to contest — the encounter happens first, on the roadside. When the plate is misread, an innocent driver becomes a felony stop in seconds. Two Greenville sisters were handcuffed at gunpoint over a car a camera wrongly flagged as stolen; a Colorado woman was accused of theft on a false hit. Due process is not supposed to arrive after the guns are drawn.

Consent of the Governed · Self-Rule

A standing army, without a vote

The founding rule: a permanent force stationed among the people requires the consent of their legislature — the very grievance the Declaration levels at the King.

Greenville's cameras went up quietly, some bought with federal forfeiture money that bypassed the normal budget. Across the Upstate there has been no public vote authorizing any of it, no ordinance setting the rules, no audit of who searches the data. The founders' deepest objection was never only that they were watched — it was that they were watched without their consent, by a power they could not hold to account. That is precisely the arrangement in the Upstate today.
